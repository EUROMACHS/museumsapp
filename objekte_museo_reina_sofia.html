Guernica

1937 | Oil on canvas | 349 x 777 cm | Museo Reina Sofia

Many aspects of this painting remaine a riddle, especially the meaning of the bull. It can be interpreted as a sign of brutality by the attackers as well as a sign of the threatened spain.

Here Picasso illustrates the misery of Guernicas population. Fleeing people with pain and sorrow written in their faces are piled on top of each other.

Die totbringenden Bomben sind in Picassos Werk nicht zu sehen. Die folgen der Attacke stellt der Künstler durch das brennende Haus und die gehetzten Menschen dar. No bombs or other weapons are displayed in this art piece. Instead Picasso focused on the consequences of the attack, seen as the burning house and the haunted people.

In 1937 Picasso was commissioned by the spanish republican Government to create a painting in the spanish pavilion at the world exhibition. The artwork should display the current situation in Spain, the civil war. Due to the prominent position Guernica was placed on the wall right next to the entrance of the pavilion.

Dead Birds

1912 | Oil in canvas | 46 x 65 cm | Museo Reina Sofia

Here is one of the two birds, which is typically for the cubism style fragmented into parts. Only the claws and the head of the bird are noticeable. Two feathers are positioned right next to it.

This art piece doesn't imitate the reality. Instead the modern approach of three-dimensional expressiveness of cubism is displayed.

A main subject of the cubism-movement was expressed by Picasso in this artwork: still life.

As a typical art piece of Picasso's cubistic period, different view points are expressed by the reduction of shape and colour. Monochrome colours and basic geometric forms creates a new pictorial space. Front and profile views can be seen in this painting simultaneously.

Woman in Blue

1901 | Oil on canvas | 133 x 100 cm | Museo Reina Sofia

During his short visits in Madrid Picasso painted women out of his memory. Due to this a series of art pieces originate, where women in parisian style with full, red lips are shown.

In 1901 Woman in Blue was displayed in the National Exibition of Fine Arts. It received only moderate criticism. Therefore Picasso decided not to pick his picture up again after the exhibitions closure. Only when Enrique Lafuente Ferrari (former head of the Museo de Arte Contemporáneo) found the artworke decades after, it was included to the states collection.

Head of a Woman

1910 | Oil on canvas | 61 x 50 cm | Museo Reina Sofia

The distinct parts of Fernandes Face are sliced into edges and sharp geometric shapes, which results in three-dimensionality.

Typically for the analytic cubism the colours range from brown- and grey scales with black highlights. Due to this the artist is able to concentrate on the formal structure of the dimension and the division of the facial parts.

During his stay in Horta (Portugal) Picasso found a way to display three-dimensionallity on the flat surface of the canvas. By fragmentation of the face in segments, Picasso was able to give this portrait spatiality. The sitter is Fernande Olivier, Picasso's partner and muse. Fernande inspired Picasso to a series of painting in the years of 1905 to 1912.

Figures by the Sea I

1932 | Oil on canvas | 130 x 97 cm | Museo Reina Sofia

In this painting Picassos shows the idea of creating colossal sculptures, which are placed by the Mediterranean shore. Two enormous heads kiss each other in front of a seaside scenery.

In his notebook Picasso already picked up the idea of the sculpure in 1928 with the titel Cazeba (Head). Therefore a sculptural consideration is the main underlying of this art piece.

In 1929 Picasso thought about situating colossal sculptures on seasides. The idea was to create living environments, where enormous female figures are displayed. Some of Picassos compositions are realizations of this idea on canvas, such as Figures by the Sea I.
