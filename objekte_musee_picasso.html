Maya with a Doll

1938 | Oil on canvas | 73,5 x 60 cm | Musee Picasso

Noticable is, in this potrait of Maya the doll has the real face whereas Maya's face is surreal deformed. Both heads are absurdly enlarged in comparisson to the rest of the body.

As a child Picasso was also portraid in a similar outfit.

In Maya with a Doll Picasso portrais his daughter Maya. In 1924 the artist already portraid his son Paulo in a similar way. Although Picasso's realisation of the portrait of Maya differs to the potrait of is son, both resemble each other in some aspects. Especially the wardrobe appears in both paintings, which is inspired by the sixteen-century dresses in Diego Velazquez master pieces.

Minotauromachy

1935 | Etching, reworked | 49,8 x 69,3 cm | Musee Picasso

This art piece is full of details. Yet a young girl with a candle and flowers in her hands stings out and is quite contrary to the violent scenery of the naked female bullfighter and the minotaur.

Two women with doves are noticeable. This symbol of peace is commonly appearing in Picasso's artworks.

Picasso produced this etching during a turbulent phase of his life. The troubled marriage with Olga Khokhlova and the pregnancy of his mistress nagged on him. Additionally Picasso layd his painting activity to rest. All these factors participate in Minotauromachy. For example seem to resemble the facial features of the girl with the flowers and the candle his mistress Marie-Thérèse Walter.

Portrait of Dora Maar

1937 | Oil on canvas | 92 x 65 cm | Musee Picasso

Parallel to this portrait, Picasso also was working on portraits of his other mistress, Marie-Thérèse Walter. In both paintings Picasso combined two view points: the off-set profile and the frontal view.

Dora Maar was one of Picasso's lovers. As surrealist photographer and speaker Maar had plenty in common. Both were especially interested in the evolvement of the political situation in spain at that time. Maar also documented the production of Guernica and supported Picasso in the developement of this painting where ever she could.

Olga in an Armchair

1918 | Oil on canvas | 130 x 88,8 cm | Musee Picasso

Due to the shading, which surrounds Olga almost like halo, this art pieces seems unfinished. Picasso created a painting with a similar background in 1914 with the title The Artist and his Model.

Almost statue-like Olga is seated with a marmoreal and doll-like face. The detailed and colourfull pattern on the background and the fan soften the dark dress of the delicate appearance of Olga.

Olga Koklova is Picasso's first wife. As a ballet dancer of the Ballet Russes Ensemble Olga traveled around Europe. Picasso followed the ensemble and came in touch with high society. After their honeymoon in Biarritz, both moved into an glamorous district of Paris.

Claude drawing, Françoise and Paloma

1954 | Oil on canvas | 116 x 89 cm | Musee Picasso

Ghost-like Françoise is hunched over both children. While Claude is drawing a picture, Paloma seems sad. Françoise hand lies comforting on Palomas shoulder.

In this painting Picasso portraited his partner and painter Françoise Gilot and their son Claude and daugther Paloma. Françoise Gilot endet the relationship with Picasso in 1953. She was the only woman in Picasso's life, who left him by her own decision.

Self Portrait

1901 | Oil on canvas | 81 x 60 cm | Musee Picasso

This painting is not a sentimental approach to illustrate himself. However, it shows the tibulations of his life without loosing his pride.

Although only 20 years of age, Picasso painted himself considerably older.

In his career as an artist Picasso often painted himself. These self portraits diplay the progress and evolvement in his painting style. Picasso made this self portrait in the winter of 1901 during his second stay in Paris. It can be seen as one of the first paintings of his Blue Period.
